How the Warriors Create Threes: Pace, Space, and Split Action

The offense starts before the ball crosses half court. With pace comes panic: defenders backpedal, matchups scramble, and one late tag turns into an open lane. The key isn’t just speed—it’s the spacing that gives speed somewhere to go.

In the half court, the system leans on split action. A big catches at the elbow, two guards cut off the catch, and a simple read—switch, trail, or top-lock—dictates the counter. If the defense switches, the screener slips. If it trails, the cutter curls. If it top-locks, the backdoor is free. Every option keeps the ball live and the help occupied.

When the starters rest, the second unit brings a different energy—pressure at the point of attack and fast decisions. The principles remain: open the floor, attack the weak side, and keep the ball moving. Threes come from advantages; advantages come from pace and organization.
